After exploring [Kagoshima City, Sakurajima](https://www.codyhou.com/kagoshima-sakurajima-travel-log/), and the [island of Yakushima](https://www.codyhou.com/yakushima-travel-log/), I spent a day traveling in the Kirishima Mountains, a volcanic area which hugs the border between Kagoshima and Miyazaki Prefectures. You can view a map which includes all the places I mention in this post [here](https://www.google.com/maps/d/edit?mid=1HTmZVqrUUHrHqJCeZXfoW0QdWnPTZ0M&usp=sharing).

## Day 8

### Getting There

The Kirishima Mountains are best accessed by rental car, as public transportation in this area is infrequent. After carb-loading myself with the excellent breakfast at the Toyoko Inn, I picked up my reservation at the [Budget Rent-a-Car Kagoshima Chuo branch](https://www.budgetrentacar.co.jp/shop/kagoshima/0005/). There are many big-name car rental companies from abroad which do business in Japan, though I'd recommend making reservations directly through the Japanese branches as it's a lot cheaper that way. I rented for 3.5 days in a Daihatsu Rocky SUV, which costed me ¥39590 ($250 at the time), including insurance. I felt much safer paying for insurance as a) I am mainly used to driving on the right, not left; b) Japanese roads are a lot skinnier; c) my American car insurance policy certainly was not going to cover an accident abroad; and d) if an accident happened, I would be exempt from fees called NOC (non-operation charge), which is compensation for lost profit over the period a damaged car is inoperable.

Since I was going to be driving on highways and for longer distances, I opted for the larger compact SUV, rather than the kei car that I had on Yakushima. I also paid for ETC car rental to make entering and exiting highways easy. It really paid off to have several days' worth of driving practice in Yakushima, since I had to navigate around bit of traffic around 8 AM rush hour, and later on had to navigate some narrow roads.

### Kirishima Jingu

My first stop was one of the oldest Shinto shrines in Japan, Kirishima Jingu, approximately an hours' drive from Kagoshima City. It's a nice shrine nestled in the foothills of the mountains. While not a necessary stop, it was a nice spot for a peaceful morning stroll. After praying at the shrine I got a _goshuin_ from here.

{% include figure image_path="/assets/images/posts/2026-02-26-kirishima-travel-log-2.jpg" alt="Kirishima Jingu" caption="Visitors line up for morning prayer." %}

### Ebino Plateau

The Kirishima Mountains are a volcanically active mountain range. While there are areas demarcated as safe to hike by the local government, conditions can change rapidly. Make travel plans and proceed at your own risk!
{: .notice--warning}

Next, I drove further into the highlands before arriving at the Ebino Plateau (えびの高原), a basin surrounded by (currently active!) volcanoes. I paid ¥500 to park and then set off on the trail to climb Mt. Karakuni (韓国岳). The air in this area was notably cooler and crisper than in Kagoshima where I started, particularly as my elevation rose, so I was glad to have my base layers.

{% include figure image_path="/assets/images/posts/2026-02-26-kirishima-travel-log-3.jpg" alt="Mt. Karakuni" caption="The view of Mt. Karakuni at the beginning of the hike, with some suspicious gases rising on the left of the frame." %}

The first attraction that caught my attention, both by sight and smell, was Ioyama, the newest volcano in the range. This can be easily identified by the billowing vents of volcanic gases with the characteristic smell of sulfur.

{% include video id="1gzF3uDQDwrJm93eMBOpItfYhQfbQmBuq" provider="google-drive" %}

After this first viewpoint, the hike gradually ascends, giving a better view of the cratered landscape marred by past volcanic activity. The tree-covered terrain with soil and boulders slowly makes way to grassland with loose volcanic rocks.

{% include figure image_path="/assets/images/posts/2026-02-26-kirishima-travel-log-4.jpg" alt="Ioyama" caption="In the distance, I tried to make out the Aso caldera, which would be my destination in a couple of days' time." %}

The vibrant blue waters of Onamino Pond presented itself as I climbed further, with the backdrop of Sakurajima in Kagoshima Bay. From here it is much more evident that Sakurajima lies on the edge of a circular body of water, which is actually the [Aira Caldera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aira_Caldera), the remnant of a supervolcano eruption. Spoopy.

{% include figure image_path="/assets/images/posts/2026-02-26-kirishima-travel-log-5.jpg" alt="Sakurajima and Onamino Pond" caption="Yet again Sakurajima belching ash into the sky. I wonder if that was contributing to the haze." %}

The trail gets a bit more tricky to hike as it's loose volcanic rocks which displace under weight, not dissimilar to hiking on loose sand, except the rock particles are larger and pointier. As I neared the top, Mt. Karakuni's crater came into view, which feels quite a bit larger and deeper than the pictures might show.

{% include gallery id="gallery_karakunidake_crater" caption="This crater is still considered part of an active volcano, by the way." %}

The peak has breathtaking views of Onamino Pond and Sakurajima as mentioned earlier, as well as a glimpse of the other volcanoes in this range. At 1700 meters high, Mt. Karakuni is only 91 meters from being the highest point on the main Kyushu island (the highest point in all of the Kyushu region would go to Mt. Miyanoura on Yakushima at 1936 meters, which I unfortunately did not get a chance to try climbing on this trip). Mt. Karakuni is one of the _Hyakumeizan_ (百名山), or 100 Famous Mountains of Japan, and my second one after previously climbing [Mt. Tsukuba](https://www.codyhou.com/six-tokyo-daytrips/#mt-tsukuba). 

{% include figure image_path="/assets/images/posts/2026-02-26-kirishima-travel-log-8.jpg" alt="Peak" caption="It was thought that the mountain was so high you could see Korean peninsula from the peak, hence the curious kanji for Korea (韓国, pronounced _karakuni_ not _kankoku_ in this context) in the mountain's name, although I'm pretty sure that claim is false." %}

To the east, the active Shinmoedake can be seen, and evidence of recent eruption and volcanic gases can be seen around its crater. In fact, the Shinmoedake crater erupted with volcanic ash as recently as 2025-09-08, as of this post. The Kirishima Ridge trail follows the course of the other volcanoes but is off-limits given the activity.

{% include video id="12uIK5edc0qewYe3Qh7yNZj1aGSnMA2JL" provider="google-drive" %}

After munching down a lunch of convenience store egg sandwiches and gummies, I decided to head down a separate trail to Onamino Pond for a hike around its perimeter. Compared to the trail from the Ebino Kogen parking lot, the descent to Onamino felt a lot steeper. There are some areas with wooden steps, although these were degraded in many places, and the muddy soil in some places didn't help either.

{% include figure image_path="/assets/images/posts/2026-02-26-kirishima-travel-log-9.jpg" alt="Steps to Onamino Pond" caption="Twisty, curvy steps." %}

I hiked Onamino Pond clockwise; the eastern side of the hike is better in my opinion, as there are many more clearings through the trees and bush to peer at the crystal blue waters with Mt. Karakuni in the background. It is also a lot less muddy than the western side. If wanting to just hike around Onamino Pond, there is a parking lot with a trailhead from Kagoshima Prefectural Route 1 with about a 30-40 minute hiking time to the pond, although it appears this lot is small and fills quickly.

{% include figure image_path="/assets/images/posts/2026-02-26-kirishima-travel-log-10.jpg" alt="Onamino Pond" caption="The intense blue water is because the water is extremely clear and also quite deep at up to 11.6 meters. I used a circular polarizer to further accentuate this against the color of the sky." %}

The remainder of the hike through the woods back to the parking lot was boring and uneventful compared to a lot of the hikes I had done thus far. You can see my hike data for Mt. Karakuni and Onamino Pond [here](https://yamap.com/activities/38272442). 

When I got back there was still time before the parking lot closed. I asked the parking lot attendant and he recommended the nearby Shiratoriyama (白鳥山). This hike is relatively easy, and is even paved for an initial short section up to a wooden outlook with nice views. I continued past this, encountering some wild deer on my way up before reaching the top. 

{% include figure image_path="/assets/images/posts/2026-02-26-kirishima-travel-log-11.jpg" alt="Shiratoriyama" caption="The water here, Byakushi Pond, is not quite as blue as that in Onamino Pond but still very reflective of the surroundings and quite photogenic." %}

The view up here was quite nice. Looking back, I actually think it would have been more enjoyable to hike around the other volcanic ponds on this side, such as Rokkannonmi Pond (六観音御池) and Fudou Pond (不動池), rather than hiking around the entirety of Onamino Pond and trudging through the woods. I took my time on the descent to enjoy the golden hour before returning to the parking lot and heading to my hotel. You can see my brief hike data [here](https://yamap.com/activities/38273963).

### Kirishima Onsen

After hiking around Yakushima and now Kirishima, my feet were quite tired and painful, and I was looking forward to soaking in some warm onsen. Actually, this was to be my first onsen experience, so I was quite excited!

When I pulled up to my accomodation for the night, Kirishima Hotel, in my crunchy, dusty hiking outfit and boots, I felt _super_ out of place; the grounds of the hotel were very well kept, and a bellman came up to help carry my belongings up to my room, something I was not _at all_ used to. Clearly there was an air of elegance which I didn't match. Oops...

I got my belongings settled and attended dinner, which was a delicious multi-course meal of seasonal vegetables, fresh fish, and meats that you cooked in front of yourself. The cuisine here was centered around local ingredients and also celebrated the samurai Ryoma Sakamoto who stayed at this hotel. One of the items was a kurobuta shamo-nabe which Sakamoto apparently ate during his stay at the hotel in 1866.

{% include figure image_path="/assets/images/posts/2026-02-26-kirishima-travel-log-13.jpg" alt="Kirishima Dinner" caption="The kurobuta shamo-nabe (黒豚軍鶏鍋) is in the upper right." %}

After the delicious meal I headed off to the onsen. The onsen is unlike anything else I've been to after, and is totally worth the stay. There are certain baths which are exclusive to either men or women, and then a giant theme park area which switches exclusivity between men and women depending on the time of day. If not staying at Kirishima Hotel these baths can also be accessed during the day hours from 11:00 to 17:00 as a _higaeri_ for only ¥1200. As someone who loves a good hot shower, I had the time of my life trying out the different baths. Just to name a few examples, one area is like a pool and goes to 1.4 meters in depth, another is cool enough and designed with special seats to be able to sleep in, and two other areas have sulfur-rich and iron-rich waters which are interesting to try.

The hotel has well-tended gardens and grounds outside to explore, but as it was getting late, I instead went to the indoor relaxing room where you can drink beverages while viewing 100-year-old cedar trees. Nothing amazing when compared to the thousands-year-old _yakusugi_ I just saw, but nonetheless a relaxing place before bed.

All in all another amazing day in Kyushu!
